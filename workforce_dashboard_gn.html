Methodology

1. Overview

This analysis estimates the investment and annual workforce required split over two periods (Years 1–5 and 6–10). The analysis is conducted across two complementary stages: (1) Access, which covers grid extension, mini-grids, and standalone solar systems required for energy access; and (2) Generation, which includes utility-scale generation capacity expansion. We combine model outputs with technology- and Africa-specific employment factors and occupational shares to produce FTE estimates by technology, phase, skill tier, and job role. The analysis accounts for construction, installation, operations, and maintenance of required assets across transmission, distribution, and generation for the 10 year period.

2. Data Sources

Data for the analysis is drawn from multiple streams, organized by stage:

2.1 Access Stage

World Bank Global Electrification Platform (GEP)

Provides investment costs, capacity additions (MW), and grid expansion (KM) by technology and year from OnSSET-based least-cost planning models to achieve universal energy access (GEP 2025).

Table 1: Access Technologies

Providing least-cost energy access across Africa requires the following technologies:

Technology Description Deployment Context
Grid Extension Transmission & distribution lines (LV/MV/HV) On-grid, national scale
Mini-grid Hydro Small-scale hydropower systems Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid Wind Hybrid Small-scale wind with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid PV Hybrid Small-scale solar with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Standalone Solar Solar Home Systems (SHS) Off-grid, single-home applications

2.2 Generation Stage

System Planning and Least-cost Analytics Tool (SPLAT)

Provides projections of utility-scale generation capacity additions by technology required to meet increased electricity demand from country-specific master plans (IRENA 2019a). SPLAT models the optimal generation mix to meet the aggregate demand of the entire power system, incorporating the additional load required to achieve universal access targets.

Note: Reference or Planned scenarios are used for all countries. In many cases, generation planning is conducted at the regional Power Pool level (e.g., WAPP, EAPP, SAPP) to capture cross-border trade and efficiencies. Where countries appear in multiple, averages are used. In the SPLAT model, the cumulative investments until 2025 are used for Years 1-5, and cumulative investments from 2025 to 2030 are used for Years 6-10. Planed investments beyond 2030 are not considered.

2.3 Employment Factors (Jobs per MW or KM)

  • Grid Extension: Derived from Australian renewable energy infrastructure employment studies (Rutovitz et al. 2025), validated against past projects and projections in South Africa (NBI 2024).
  • Mini-grids: Calculated from Power for All (2022), which is based on direct surveys across Kenya, Ethiopia, Nigeria, and Uganda. Splits between construction/installation and operations/maintenance are sourced from IRENA (2024) case study on Kenyan mini-grids.
  • Solar Home Systems: Calculated from GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.
  • Grid-Scale Generation: Sourced from Rutovitz et al. (2025) for utility-scale solar, wind, hydro, gas, and coal technologies. Values are adjusted for Sub-Saharan African context using productivity multipliers from Ram et al. (2020): 5.00× for Years 1-5 and 4.54× for Years 6-10.

2.4 Occupational Shares (Job Distribution by Role)

  • Grid Extension, Mini-grids, and Generation: Sourced from Australian infrastructure occupational shares (Rutovitz et al. 2025), with utility-scale solar shares assumed to approximate mini-grid PV and grid-scale generation technologies.
  • Solar Home Systems (SHS): Mapped using role-specific breakdowns provided in GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.

3. Workforce Requirement Calculation

The workforce calculation follows a standardized approach across both stages:

Step 1. Estimate infrastructure needed

Access: For each 5-year period (Years 1-5 and Years 6-10):

  • Grid Extension: Sum of line additions (KM) by voltage level (LV, MV, HV)
  • Mini-grids & Standalone Solar: Sum of new capacity additions (MW) by technology

Generation: For each 5-year period:

  • Years 1-5 Capacity: Total utility-scale generation capacity (MW) required (sourced from SPLAT cumulative 2025 projections)
  • Years 6-10 Capacity: Additional capacity (MW) required (sourced from SPLAT incremental 2025-2030 projections)

Step 2. Compute workforce needed

The core formula for calculating workforce needs is consistent across stages. Note that regional productivity adjustments for generation are already incorporated into the employment factors used.

Annual Jobs Required = Capacity Required × Employment Factor × Occupational Share

Where:

  • Capacity Required: KM of lines for grid extension or MW of capacity for generation/mini-grids/standalone solar
  • Employment Factor: Technology-specific jobs per unit (jobs/MW or jobs/KM), adjusted for regional productivity
  • Occupational Share: Proportion of total jobs in each role category

4. Skill Level Classification

A taxonomy is built using Perplexity Deep Research to map each job role to a skill tier using the International Standard Classification of Occupations (ISCO-08) and International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED-11) frameworks.

Step 1: Job Role Identification

50 job roles across the energy access and generation value chain are identified:

  • Grid Extension, Mini-grids, and Generation: Occupational categories from Rutovitz et al. (2025), including roles in transmission & distribution, and utility-scale solar, wind, hydro, gas, and coal
  • Solar Home Systems: Role-specific breakdowns from GOGLA (2024) employer surveys across West, Central, and East Africa
  • Coverage: Managers, engineers, professionals, technicians, trades, machine operators, administrative staff, and laborers

Step 2: ISCO-08 Occupational Mapping with AI

Each of the 50 job roles is fuzzy matched to the closest ISCO-08 unit group. This provided:

  • Standardized occupational definitions recognized internationally
  • Associated ISCO skill levels (1–4) for each role
  • Consistent framework for comparing roles across different studies and contexts

Step 3: ISCED Level Assignment with AI

ISCO skill levels are mapped to single ISCED-11 education level, combining standard ILO/UNESCO correspondence with African evidence:

  • Initial Band Mapping: ISCO skill levels converted to ISCED bands using standard correspondence (Skill 1→ISCED 1–2, Skill 2→ISCED 3–4, Skill 3→ISCED 5, Skill 4→ISCED 6–8)
  • African Evidence Validation: Each role validated against concrete African qualification requirements from national qualifications frameworks (SAQA in South Africa, KNQA/TVETA in Kenya), government/utility job specifications (Kenya Power, Eskom), TVET documentation, and sector job advertisements
  • Single Level Selection: Within each ISCED band, Perplexity identified the single most typical formal education level based on documented African evidence
  • Final Grouping: The resulting single ISCED levels were consolidated into four tiers based on the distribution: ISCED 1–3, ISCED 4, ISCED 5, and ISCED 6–8

5.1 Scenario Configuration

This analysis uses the following scenario configuration:

  • Demand Level: Estimated Poverty/GDP
  • Energy Use: Productive Use Included (Health, Education, Small-scale Commercial)
  • Carbon Pricing: No Carbon Pricing
  • PV Cost Assumption: Expected PV Costs
  • Connection Capacity: Capped Annual Connections

5.4 Occupational Shares

The occupation shares within each job (managers, engineers, technicians, etc.) is specified by Rutovitz et al (2025) for grid extension, utility-scale solar, onshore wind, hydro, gas, and coal. The same occupational shares are used across both Access and Generation stages for comparable technologies (e.g., utility-scale solar shares are used for mini-grid PV and grid-scale solar).

For standalone solar, GOGLA (2024) provides a general occupational breakdown, which is mapped to specific skill categories and phases for the SHS workforce. GOGLA roles that cover both C&I and O&M are split 50/50 between each phase. This results in a total C&I share of 82.3%, in line with the mini-grid figure of 77.2%.

5.5 Annualization

Critical distinction in workforce calculations:

  • Construction & Installation (C&I): Total job-years are divided by the 5-year construction period to get annual FTE requirements. For example, if building 10 MW of Hydro capacity requires 1,000 job-years, this translates to 200 FTE/year over 5 years.
  • Operations & Maintenance (O&M): Already expressed as annual jobs per MW of installed capacity: these are ongoing positions for the lifetime of the asset. For Years 6-10, O&M jobs reflect the cumulative installed capacity from both periods (Years 1-5 + Years 6-10).

6. Key Assumptions & Limitations

Assumptions

  • Employment factors do not fall with time (low productivity increase scenario).
  • Construction period is 5 years for each capacity addition phase as modeled by GEP (2025) and IRENA (2019a).
  • Occupational distributions remain stable across the projection period.
  • Regional productivity adjustments for Generation stage (5.00× for Years 1-5, 4.54× for Years 6-10) are applied uniformly across all Sub-Saharan African countries.
  • Country-specific generation investment costs are used when available; otherwise global averages are applied.

Limitations

  • Employment factors and skill tiers are Africa-specific, but may not fully capture local labor market conditions within individual African countries.
  • Manufacturing jobs are excluded (focus on deployment only).
  • The analysis assumes generation capacity additions occur to meet demand from universal access; actual generation expansion may differ based on existing capacity and demand growth rates.
  • Productivity multipliers are based on regional averages and may not reflect country-specific conditions.
