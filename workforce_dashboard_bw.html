1. Overview

This analysis estimates the investment and annual workforce required to achieve key energy targets across Africa over the next 10 years. The analysis is conducted across two complementary objectives: (1) Access, which covers grid extension, mini-grids, and standalone solar systems required for energy access; and (2) Generation, which includes utility-scale generation capacity expansion. We combine least-cost infrastructure plans with technology- and Africa-specific employment factors and occupational shares to produce required annual workforce estimates by technology, phase, skill tier, and job role. The analysis accounts for construction, installation, operations, and maintenance of required assets across transmission, distribution, and generation for the 10 year period. Finally, we connect these annual demand estimates with annual supply estimates from nationally-representative labor force surveys to estimate supply-demand gaps.

2. Data Sources

Data for the analysis is drawn from multiple streams, organized by stage:

2.1 Access Stage

World Bank Global Electrification Platform (GEP)

Provides investment costs, capacity additions (MW), and grid expansion (KM) by technology and year from OnSSET-based least-cost planning models to achieve universal energy access (GEP 2025).

2.2 Generation Stage

System Planning and Least-cost Analytics Tool (SPLAT)

Provides projections of utility-scale generation capacity additions by technology required to meet increased electricity demand from country-specific master plans (IRENA 2019a). SPLAT models the optimal generation mix to meet the aggregate demand of the entire power system, incorporating the additional load required to achieve universal access targets.

Note: Reference or Planned scenarios are used for all countries. In many cases, generation planning is conducted at the regional Power Pool level (e.g., WAPP, EAPP, SAPP) to capture cross-border trade and efficiencies. Where countries appear in multiple, averages are used. In the SPLAT model, the cumulative investments until 2025 are used for Years 1-5, and cumulative investments from 2025 to 2030 are used for Years 6-10. Planned investments beyond 2030 are not considered.

2.3 Employment Factors (Jobs per MW or KM)

  • Grid Extension: Derived from Australian renewable energy infrastructure employment studies (Rutovitz et al. 2025), validated against past projects and projections in South Africa (NBI 2024).
  • Mini-grids: Calculated from Power for All (2022), which is based on direct surveys across Kenya, Ethiopia, Nigeria, and Uganda. Splits between construction/installation and operations/maintenance are sourced from IRENA (2024) case study on Kenyan mini-grids.
  • Solar Home Systems: Calculated from GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.
  • Grid-Scale Generation: Sourced from Rutovitz et al. (2025) for utility-scale solar, wind, hydro, gas, and coal technologies. Values are adjusted for Sub-Saharan African context using productivity multipliers from Ram et al. (2020): 5.00× for Years 1-5 and 4.54× for Years 6-10.

2.4 Occupational Shares (Job Distribution by Role)

  • Grid Extension, Mini-grids, and Generation: Sourced from Australian infrastructure occupational shares (Rutovitz et al. 2025), with utility-scale solar shares assumed to approximate mini-grid PV and grid-scale generation technologies.
  • Solar Home Systems (SHS): Mapped using role-specific breakdowns provided in GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.

3. Workforce Requirement Calculation

The workforce calculation follows a standardized approach across both stages:

Step 1. Estimate infrastructure needed

Access: For each 5-year period (Years 1-5 and Years 6-10):

  • Grid Extension: Sum of line additions (KM) by voltage level (LV, MV, HV)
  • Mini-grids & Standalone Solar: Sum of new capacity additions (MW) by technology

Generation: For each 5-year period:

  • Years 1-5 Capacity: Total utility-scale generation capacity (MW) required
  • Years 6-10 Capacity: Additional capacity (MW) required

Step 2. Compute workforce needed

Annual Jobs Required = Capacity Required × Employment Factor × Occupational Share

Where:

  • Capacity Required: KM of lines or MW of capacity
  • Employment Factor: Technology-specific jobs per unit (jobs/MW or jobs/KM)
  • Occupational Share: Proportion of total jobs in each role category

4. Skill Level Classification

A taxonomy is built using Perplexity Deep Research to map each job role to a skill tier using the International Standard Classification of Occupations (ISCO-08) and International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED-11) frameworks.

Step 1: Job Role Identification

50 job roles across the energy access and generation value chain are identified:

  • Grid Extension, Mini-grids, and Generation: Occupational categories from Rutovitz et al. (2025)
  • Solar Home Systems: Role-specific breakdowns from GOGLA (2024)
  • Coverage: Managers, engineers, professionals, technicians, trades, machine operators, administrative staff, and laborers

Step 2: ISCO-08 Occupational Mapping with AI

Each of the 50 job roles is fuzzy matched to the closest ISCO-08 unit group, providing standardized occupational definitions and associated skill levels.

Step 3: ISCED Level Assignment with AI

ISCO skill levels are mapped to single ISCED-11 education levels combining standard ILO/UNESCO correspondence with African evidence:

  • Initial Band Mapping: ISCO skill levels converted to ISCED bands using standard correspondence
  • African Evidence Validation: Each role validated against national qualifications frameworks (SAQA, KNQA/TVETA), utility job specifications, and sector advertisements
  • Final Grouping: Consolidated into four tiers: ISCED 1–3, ISCED 4, ISCED 5, and ISCED 6–8

5.4 Occupational Shares

Occupational shares within each job are specified by Rutovitz et al (2025) for grid extension and utility-scale generation. The same shares are used across comparable technologies. For standalone solar, GOGLA (2024) provides a general occupational breakdown mapped to specific skill categories and phases.

5.5 Annualization

  • Construction & Installation (C&I): Total job-years are divided by the 5-year construction period to get annual FTE requirements.
  • Operations & Maintenance (O&M): Already expressed as annual jobs per MW of installed capacity. For Years 6-10, O&M reflects cumulative installed capacity from both periods.

6. Supply-Demand Gap Analysis

The workforce demand estimates from sections 3–5 are compared against an empirical estimate of the existing labour supply available to the energy sector in each country using the associated ISCO code for each job role. The gap identifies where the current workforce pipeline falls short of projected energy sector needs, and is defined as:

Annual Gap (Workers/Year) = Annual Supply − Annual Demand

Annual Supply = Relevant Workforce × Annual Entrant Rate

A positive gap indicates oversupply; a negative gap indicates undersupply. The % Demand Unmet shown in the chart title is the sum of shortfalls across all roles with unmet demand divided by total demand — surpluses in one role do not offset deficits in another.

6.1 Labour Supply Data Sources

World Bank Global Labor Database (GLD)

For most countries, supply is estimated from the World Bank's Global Labor Database (GLD), which provides harmonized, nationally-representative household survey microdata. The GLD standardizes occupation codes across country-specific surveys. Countries are included only when the effective sample exceeds 2,000 respondents with occupation codes.

IPUMS International (Malawi and Mozambique)

Supplements the GLD by providing harmonized census and survey microdata for countries where GLD coverage is absent or insufficient. Standardized broad industry categories (e.g., Mining, Manufacturing, Utilities, Construction) are used to isolate energy-adjacent workers. 3-digit ISCO occupational codes are recorded and subsequently upscaled proportionally to 4-digit codes via the regional SSA reference distribution.

Country-Specific Labour Force Surveys (Nigeria and Tanzania)

Tanzania and Nigeria provide full microdata with reliable 4-digit ISCO occupational coding and 4-digit ISIC industry coding, and are used as primary sources for both supply estimation and the empirical new entrant rate computation.

  • Tanzania ILFS 2024: 4-digit ISCO-88 occupational codes, 4-digit ISIC industry codes, and job start year. Converted to ISCO-08 using the ILO official correspondence table.
  • Nigeria NLFS 2024 Q2: 1-digit ISCO major group labels and job start year.

6.2 Identifying the Relevant Workforce

Not all required job roles require workers who are currently employed in energy industries. The analysis distinguishes two categories:

  • Energy-specific roles — occupations whose skills are largely sector-specific and whose supply should be drawn from workers already employed in energy and construction industries (e.g. Electricians, Lineworkers, Civil Engineers, Control Room Operators, SHS Technicians). For these roles, supply is restricted to workers identified as being in energy-adjacent ISIC industries.
  • Generalist roles — occupations that serve many sectors and whose labour pool is not energy-specific (e.g. Truck Drivers, Admin Staff, General Labourers, IT Professionals, Executives). For these roles, supply is drawn from the full working population, since an employer can recruit from any sector.

For Nigeria and Tanzania, energy-specific workers are identified using the 4-digit ISIC industry code recorded directly in each survey, keeping workers in ISIC divisions 05–39 (Mining and Manufacturing) and 41–43 (Construction and utilities). For GLD countries, the industry column may contain numeric ISIC codes, ISIC section letters, or free-text descriptions depending on the survey. A multi-rule classifier handles all three formats:

  • Numeric prefix: Two-digit ISIC codes 05–39 and 41–43 flagged as energy/construction.
  • ISIC section letters: Sections B (Mining), C (Manufacturing), D (Electricity/gas), E (Water/waste), F (Construction) flagged.
  • Text descriptions: English and French keyword matching for construction, electrical, manufacturing, mining, petroleum, hydro, infrastructure, and related terms. The keyword oil is restricted to petroleum-specific phrases (crude oil, oil well, oil field) to avoid falsely flagging agricultural industries such as oil seeds or palm oil.

Where the industry column has fewer than 5% non-missing values — indicating a survey design that does not consistently record industry — the energy filter is bypassed and all workers are used as the supply base regardless of role type.

6.4 Occupational Code Upscaling

When surveys record occupational codes at coarser than 4-digit precision, a hierarchical upscaling procedure distributes workers across 4-digit ISCO-08 sub-groups using an SSA reference distribution built from all GLD countries with 4-digit codes, including Tanzania and Nigeria.

  • 3-digit codes: Allocated across all 4-digit unit groups within that sub-major group in proportion to SSA reference shares.
  • 2-digit and 1-digit codes: Allocated similarly at minor and major group level.
  • Zero-padding correction: Some surveys store ISCO sub-major codes zero-padded to 4 characters (e.g. '7100' for sub-major 71). Trailing zeros are stripped before measuring digit depth.
  • ISCO-88 conversion: Surveys coded in ISCO-88 (e.g. Kenya KCHS) are first converted to ISCO-08 using the ILO official correspondence table. Codes absent from the table but present in the SSA reference distribution are treated as identity-mapped.

6.5 Demand-Weighted Supply Allocation

Each 4-digit ISCO-08 code may map to multiple job roles. Workers are allocated across roles in proportion to the relative FTE demand for each role — ensuring high-demand roles receive proportionally more of the available supply signal. Energy-specific roles draw supply from energy-sector workers only; generalist roles draw from the full working population.

6.6 Demographic Scaling (Current Gap Alignment)

To ensure an 'apples-to-apples' comparison between projected near-term workforce demand (Years 1-5, representing ~2025 targets) and historical labor supply survey data (often ranging from 2014 to 2022), supply estimates are dynamically scaled to the target year (2025).

Using the World Bank's World Development Indicators (WDI) API, the ratio of the target year population to the survey year population is calculated for each country. This demographic multiplier is applied to the raw survey workforce estimates—assuming constant labor force participation and occupational distribution rates—to yield the present-day labor supply available to meet the projected demand.

6.7 Country-Specific Adjustments

  • Kenya: Three KCHS survey rounds (2020, 2021, 2022) are pooled to reach an adequate sample of 34,000+ respondents. Weights are divided by three to preserve a single-year population estimate. Energy-specific supply is scaled by Tanzania's observed energy sector share within each ISCO major group.
  • Countries with insufficient data: Countries with fewer than 2,000 occupation-coded respondents are excluded; no gap chart is produced.

7. Key Assumptions & Limitations

Assumptions

  • Employment factors do not fall with time (exception for the generation stage, as specified below).
  • Regional productivity adjustments (5.00× for Years 1-5, 4.54× for Years 6-10) are applied uniformly to the generation stage.
  • Construction period is 5 years for each capacity addition phase.
  • Occupational distributions remain stable across the projection period.
  • New entrant rates derived from Tanzania and Nigeria are applied to all SSA countries in the gap analysis.
  • Occupational upscaling via the SSA reference distribution assumes the regional occupational mix is a reasonable approximation for individual countries.

Limitations

  • Employment factors and skill tiers are Africa-specific but may not fully capture local labour market conditions within individual countries.
  • Manufacturing jobs are excluded (focus on deployment only).
  • GLD surveys vary in recency (2009–2024); older surveys may not reflect current occupational structures.
