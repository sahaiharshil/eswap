Methodology

1. Overview

This analysis estimates the investment and annual workforce required to achieve full household electrification in Africa over a 10-year program (split over two periods, Years 1–5 and 6–10). It integrates least-cost capacity and grid expansion outputs from the Global Electrification Platform (GEP) with technology- and Africa-specific employment factors and occupational shares to produce FTE estimates by technology, phase, skill tier, and job role. This accounts for construction, installation, operations, and maintenance of required assets for the 10 year period.

2. Technology Grouping

Least-cost energy access infrastructure deployment across Africa is largely driven by four main technology groups:

Technology Group Included Technologies Deployment Context
Standalone Solar Solar Home Systems (SHS) Off-grid, single-home applications
Mini-grid PV Hybrid Small-scale solar with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid Wind Hybrid Small-scale wind with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid Hydro Small-scale hydropower systems Off-grid, community-level applications
Grid Extension Transmission & distribution lines (LV/MV/HV) On-grid, national scale

3. Data Sources

Data for the analysis is drawn from three main streams: Global Electrification Platform (GEP) outputs for capacity/line additions, and technology-specific studies for Employment Factors & Occupational Shares.

3.1 Word Bank Global Electrification Platform (GEP)

Provides investment costs, capacity additions (MW), and grid expansion (KM) by technology and year from OnSSET-based least-cost planning models to achieve universal energy access.

3.2 Employment Factors (Jobs per MW or KM)

  • T&D Lines: Derived from Australian renewable energy infrastructure employment studies (Rutovitz et al. 2025), validated against past projects and projections in South Africa (NBI 2024).
  • Solar Home Systems & Minigrids: Calculated from Power for All (2022), which is based on direct surveys across Kenya, Ethiopia, Nigeria, and Uganda.

3.3 Occupational Shares (Job Distribution by Role)

  • T&D Lines & Minigrids: Sourced primarily from Australian infrastructure occupational shares, with utility-scale solar shares assumed to approximate minigrid PV.
  • Solar Home Systems (SHS): Mapped using the GOGLA (2018) regional workforce breakdown (for sales, management, etc.), supplemented by IRENA (2024) survey data for construction/O&M splits.

4. Calculation Methodology

4.1 Estimate infrastructure needed to reach 100% access

For each 5-year period (Years 1-5 and Years 6-10):

  • Generation: Sum of new capacity additions (MW) by technology
  • Grid: Sum of line additions (KM) by voltage level (LV, MV, HV)

4.2 Compute workforce needed to build required infrastructure

The core formula for calculating workforce needs:

Annual Jobs Required = Capacity Required × Employment Factor × Occupational Share

Where:

  • Capacity Required: MW for generation or KM for grid infrastructure
  • Employent Factor: Technology-specific jobs per unit (jobs/MW or jobs/KM)
  • Occupational Share: Proportion of total jobs in each role category

5. Skill Level Classification

Jobs are categorized into five skill tiers based on required qualifications and responsibilities:

Skill Tier Key Roles Required Qualification/Experience
Low: Laborers & Admin General Laborers, Construction Labourers, Admin Staff, Storepersons, SHS Customer Service Basic education
Low-Medium: Machine Operators & Sales Truck Drivers, Machine Operators, SHS Sales & Distribution Vocational training / on-the-job experience
Medium: Trades & Technicians Electricians, Lineworkers, Plumbers, Metal Trades, Technicians, SHS Technicians Technical training / certification and trades experience
Medium-High: Professionals IT Professionals, Finance/Policy Professionals, Construction Managers, Operations/Production Managers, Business/Procurement Managers, Control Room Operators University degree and relevant professional experience

6. Key Assumptions & Limitations

Assumptions

  • Employment factors do not fall with time (low productivity increase scenario).
  • Construction period is 5 years for each capacity addition phase.
  • Occupational distributions remain stable across the projection period.
  • Note: No country-specific productivity adjustment: all employment factors come from or are validated against Africa-specific estimates.

Limitations

  • The model is based on infrastructure to provide energy access; generation is excluded and so this serves as a lower bound for sector-wide workforce needs.
  • Employment factors may not fully capture local labor market conditions.
  • Manufacturing jobs are excluded (focus on deployment only).
  • Does not model workforce transitions between construction and O&M phases.
  • Regional variations within countries are not captured.

7.4 Annualization

Critical distinction in workforce calculations:

  • Construction & Installation jobs: Total job-years are divided by the 5-year construction period to get annual FTE requirements. For example, if building 10 MW requires 1000 job-years, this translates to 200 FTE/year over 5 years.
  • Operations & Maintenance jobs: Already expressed as annual jobs per MW of installed capacity. These are ongoing positions for the lifetime of the asset.
  • Cumulative O&M: For Years 6-10, O&M jobs reflect the cumulative installed capacity from both periods (Years 1-5 + Years 6-10).
