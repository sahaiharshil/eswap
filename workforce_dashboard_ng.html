Methodology

1. Overview

This analysis estimates the investment and annual workforce required to achieve full household electrification in Africa over a 10-year program (split over two periods, Years 1–5 and 6–10). It integrates least-cost capacity and grid expansion outputs from the Global Electrification Platform (GEP) with technology- and Africa-specific employment factors and occupational shares to produce FTE estimates by technology, phase, skill tier, and job role. This accounts for construction, installation, operations, and maintenance of required assets for the 10 year period.

2. Data Sources

Data for the analysis is drawn from three main streams: Global Electrification Platform (GEP) outputs for capacity/line additions, and technology-specific studies for Employment Factors & Occupational Shares.

2.1 Word Bank Global Electrification Platform (GEP)

Provides investment costs, capacity additions (MW), and grid expansion (KM) by technology and year from OnSSET-based least-cost planning models to achieve universal energy access.

Table 1: Least Cost Technologies for Energy Access

Providing least-cost energy access across Africa requires four main types of technologies:

Technology Description Deployment Context
Grid Extension Transmission & distribution lines (LV/MV/HV) On-grid, national scale
Mini-grid Hydro Small-scale hydropower systems Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid Wind Hybrid Small-scale wind with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Mini-grid PV Hybrid Small-scale solar with battery/diesel backup Off-grid, community-level applications
Standalone Solar Solar Home Systems (SHS) Off-grid, single-home applications

2.2 Employment Factors (Jobs per MW or KM)

  • Grid Extension: Derived from Australian renewable energy infrastructure employment studies (Rutovitz et al. 2025), validated against past projects and projections in South Africa (NBI 2024).
  • Minigrids: Calculated from Power for All (2022), which is based on direct surveys across Kenya, Ethiopia, Nigeria, and Uganda. Splits between construction/installation and operations/maintenance are sourced from IRENA (2024) case study on Kenyan minigrids.
  • Solar Home Systems: Calculated from GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.

2.3 Occupational Shares (Job Distribution by Role)

  • Grid Extension & Minigrids: Sourced from Australian infrastructure occupational shares (Rutovitz et al. 2025), with utility-scale solar shares assumed to approximate minigrid PV.
  • Solar Home Systems (SHS): Mapped using role-specific breakdowns provided in GOGLA (2024), which is based on direct surveys across West, Central, and East Africa.

3. Workforce Requirement Calculation

Once the data is collected, two steps are needed to compute workforce needs for each country:

Step 1. Estimate infrastructure needed to reach 100% access

For each 5-year period (Years 1-5 and Years 6-10):

  • Grid Extension: Sum of line additions (KM) by voltage level (LV, MV, HV)
  • Minigrids & Standalone Solar: Sum of new capacity additions (MW) by technology

Step 2. Compute workforce needed to build required infrastructure

The core formula for calculating workforce needs:

Annual Jobs Required = Capacity Required × Employment Factor × Occupational Share

Where:

  • Capacity Required: KM of LV/MV/HV lines for grid extension or MW of capacity for minigrids & standalone solar
  • Employent Factor: Technology-specific jobs per unit (jobs/MW or jobs/KM)
  • Occupational Share: Proportion of total jobs in each role category

4. Skill Level Classification

A taxonomy is built using Perplexity Deep Research to map each job role to a skill tier using the International Standard Classification of Occupations (ISCO-08) and International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED-11) frameworks.

Step 1: Job Role Identification

50 job roles across the energy access value chain are identified:

  • Grid Extension & Minigrids: Occupational categories from Rutovitz et al. (2025), including roles in transmission & distribution, and utility-scale solar, wind, and hydro
  • Solar Home Systems: Role-specific breakdowns from GOGLA (2024) employer surveys across West, Central, and East Africa
  • Coverage: Managers, engineers, professionals, technicians, trades, machine operators, administrative staff, and laborers

Step 2: ISCO-08 Occupational Mapping with AI

Each of the 50 job roles is fuzzy matched to the closest ISCO-08 unit group. This provided:

  • Standardized occupational definitions recognized internationally
  • Associated ISCO skill levels (1–4) for each role
  • Consistent framework for comparing roles across different studies and contexts

Step 3: ISCED Level Assignment with AI

ISCO skill levels are mapped to single ISCED-11 education level, combining standard ILO/UNESCO correspondence with African evidence:

  • Initial Band Mapping: ISCO skill levels converted to ISCED bands using standard correspondence (Skill 1→ISCED 1–2, Skill 2→ISCED 3–4, Skill 3→ISCED 5, Skill 4→ISCED 6–8)
  • African Evidence Validation: Each role validated against concrete African qualification requirements from national qualifications frameworks (SAQA in South Africa, KNQA/TVETA in Kenya), government/utility job specifications (Kenya Power, Eskom), TVET documentation, and sector job advertisements
  • Single Level Selection: Within each ISCED band, Perplexity identified the single most typical formal education level based on documented African evidence
  • Final Grouping: The resulting single ISCED levels were consolidated into four tiers based on the distribution: ISCED 1–3, ISCED 4, ISCED 5, and ISCED 6–8

5.1 Scenario Configuration

This analysis uses the following scenario configuration:

  • Demand Level: Estimated Poverty/GDP
  • Energy Use: Residential Only
  • Carbon Pricing: No Carbon Pricing
  • PV Cost Assumption: Expected PV Costs
  • Connection Capacity: Capped Annual Connections

5.3 Occupational Breakdown

The occupation distribution within each job (managers, engineers, technicians, etc.) is specified by Rutovitz et al (2025) for grid extension, utility-scale solar, on-shore wind, and hydro. The latter three are used for solar hybrid minigrid, wind hybrid minigrid, and hydro minigrid, respectively.

For standalone solar, GOGLA (2024) provides a general occupational breakdown, which is mapped to specific skill categories and phases for the SHS workforce. GOGLA roles that cover both C&I and O&M are split 50/50 between each phase. This results in a total C&amp;I share of 82.3%, in line with the minigrid figure of 77.2%

5.4 Annualization

Critical distinction in workforce calculations:

  • Construction & Installation jobs: Total job-years are divided by the 5-year construction period to get annual FTE requirements. For example, if building 10 MW requires 1000 job-years, this translates to 200 FTE/year over 5 years.
  • Operations & Maintenance jobs: Already expressed as annual jobs per MW of installed capacity. These are ongoing positions for the lifetime of the asset.
  • Cumulative O&M: For Years 6-10, O&M jobs reflect the cumulative installed capacity from both periods (Years 1-5 + Years 6-10).

6. Key Assumptions & Limitations

Assumptions

  • Employment factors do not fall with time (low productivity increase scenario).
  • Construction period is 5 years for each capacity addition phase.
  • Occupational distributions remain stable across the projection period.
  • No country-specific productivity adjustment: all employment factors come from or are validated against Africa-specific estimates.

Limitations

  • The model is based on infrastructure required to provide energy access; jobs required for generation are excluded.
  • Employment factors and skill tiers are Africa-specific, but may not fully capture local labor market conditions across and within African countries.
  • Manufacturing jobs are excluded (focus on deployment only).
